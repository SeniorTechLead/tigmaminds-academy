# Why the Rhino Has Only One Horn — A Kaziranga Tale

> Long ago, all rhinos had three horns. But one curious little rhino made a deal with the river that changed everything. A folktale from Kaziranga for young readers.

## The Three-Horned Rhino

A long, long time ago — before your grandmother's grandmother was born — the rhinoceroses of Kaziranga had not one horn, but **three**. They were very proud of their three horns. They polished them on tree trunks every morning and admired their reflections in the river.

The tallest horn was for **courage**. The middle horn was for **wisdom**. And the smallest horn was for **kindness**.

## The Curious Little Rhino

Among all the rhinos in the grassland lived a little one named **Gorha**. Gorha was the most curious rhino in all of Kaziranga. While the other rhinos spent their days eating grass and napping in the mud, Gorha wanted to know everything.

"Why does the river flow only one way?" Gorha asked his mother.

"Why does the sun hide at night?" Gorha asked his father.

"Why do the elephants have such long noses?" Gorha asked his grandmother.

Nobody had answers. "Stop asking so many questions," they said. "Just eat your grass."

But Gorha couldn't stop. Questions were like an itch inside his head that only answers could scratch.

## The River's Offer

One morning, Gorha wandered to the edge of the great Brahmaputra river. The water was high and muddy, as it always was during the rains.

"River," said Gorha, "you go everywhere and see everything. Can you answer my questions?"

The river gurgled and swirled and, to Gorha's surprise, spoke: *"I can answer any question, little one. But every answer has a price. For each answer, you must give me one horn."*

Gorha looked at his three beautiful horns reflected in the water. Three horns. Three questions. It seemed like a fair deal.

## The Three Questions

"Why does the river flow only one way?" asked Gorha.

*"Because even water knows that you can only move forward in life, never back,"* said the river. And — **pop!** — Gorha's tallest horn, the horn of courage, dissolved into the water.

"Why does the sun hide at night?" asked Gorha.

*"So that you learn to find your way in the dark. Anyone can be brave in the light,"* said the river. And — **pop!** — the middle horn, the horn of wisdom, slipped away.

Gorha looked at his reflection. Only one small horn remained — the horn of kindness. He had one question left.

He thought very carefully. He thought about all the questions he had ever wanted to ask. And then he smiled.

"I don't need to ask my third question," said Gorha.

*"Why not?"* asked the river, surprised.

"Because the best question is always the next one. If I use my last question now, I'll have all answers but no more curiosity. I'd rather keep one horn and keep wondering."

## The Gift

The river was so impressed that it laughed — a great, rolling, thundering laugh that shook the banks. *"You are the wisest creature I have ever met,"* it said. *"You chose curiosity over certainty. For that, I will make your one remaining horn the strongest horn in all the animal kingdom. No tiger's claw, no hunter's trap, no storm's fury will ever break it."*

And so it was. From that day on, all the rhinos of Kaziranga had one horn — **the horn of kindness**, made unbreakable by curiosity.

And if you visit Kaziranga today and see a rhino standing still by the river, looking like it's thinking very hard — well, maybe it is. Maybe it's deciding what question to ask next.

*The end.*

# The Tea Leaf That Wanted to Fly

> In a tea garden in upper Assam, one little leaf dreamed of something no tea leaf had ever done — flying. A story about dreams, patience, and finding where you belong.

## The Smallest Leaf

In a tea garden in upper Assam, where the rows of bushes stretched like green waves all the way to the hills, there lived a tiny tea leaf named **Pahi**.

Pahi was the smallest leaf on the smallest branch of the smallest bush in the entire garden. Every morning, she watched the birds fly overhead — the bulbuls, the kingfishers, the great hornbills with their enormous beaks — and she wished with all her heart that she could fly too.

"Don't be silly," said the older leaves. "We are tea leaves. We grow, we get plucked, we become tea. That is our purpose."

"But what if my purpose is different?" said Pahi.

## The Wind's Promise

One afternoon, a warm wind blew through the garden — the kind of wind that comes just before the monsoon, carrying the smell of rain and faraway places.

"Wind!" called Pahi. "Can you teach me to fly?"

The wind rustled through the tea bushes and whispered: *"I cannot teach a leaf to fly, little one. But I can teach you to let go."*

"What's the difference?" asked Pahi.

*"Flying is for those with wings. Letting go is for those with courage. And sometimes, letting go takes you further than any wing ever could."*

## The Plucking

The next morning, the tea pluckers came — women in colourful mekhela chadors, their fingers moving quick as hummingbirds, picking only the youngest, tenderest leaves.

And they plucked Pahi.

The older leaves had always said this was the end. But for Pahi, it felt like a beginning.

She was carried in a basket with a hundred other leaves, taken to the factory where she was rolled and dried and transformed. She was no longer green and fresh. She was dark and curled and fragrant — she had become **tea**.

## The Journey

Pahi was packed into a box, loaded onto a truck, put on a train, transferred to a ship. She crossed rivers wider than the tea garden, mountains taller than the tallest tree, and an ocean so vast that even the wind seemed small above it.

And then one winter morning, in a country called England, a woman opened the box, spooned Pahi into a cup, and poured hot water over her.

Pahi felt herself dissolving — her flavour, her warmth, her essence rising as steam. And the steam rose and rose, past the cup, past the table, past the window, up into the cold English sky.

She was flying.

Not with wings. Not like a bird. But as something better — as warmth, as comfort, as the taste of a faraway place carried across the world.

*"The wind was right,"* thought Pahi. *"Letting go took me further than any wing ever could."*

*The end.*

# How the Hoolock Gibbon Got Its Song

> The forests of Northeast India are filled with the morning songs of the hoolock gibbon. But do you know how they first learned to sing? A story from the treetops.

## The Silent Forest

Once upon a time, the forests of Northeast India were completely **silent**. No birdsong. No frog calls. No insect hum. The animals could see and smell and run and climb, but none of them could make a single sound.

This made the forest a lonely place. The elephants walked in silence. The deer ran without a rustle. And the hoolock gibbons — the small, furry apes who lived in the tallest trees — swung from branch to branch without a whisper.

## The Moon's Gift

One night, the Moon looked down at the silent forest and felt sad. "A forest without sound is like a river without water," she said. "I must give them music."

The Moon gathered all the sounds she could find — the splash of rain, the crack of thunder, the whisper of clouds, the rush of rivers — and rolled them into a single, shining ball of sound. She dropped it into the forest just before dawn.

The ball of sound landed in the highest tree — right next to a sleeping hoolock gibbon named **Huhu**.

## The First Song

Huhu woke up and found the glowing ball beside him. He touched it, and it burst — sending sounds flying in every direction like seeds in the wind.

Some sounds landed on the birds and became birdsong. Some fell on the frogs and became croaking. Some scattered onto the crickets and became chirping. Some splashed into the rivers and became the sound of flowing water.

But the biggest, most beautiful piece of sound stayed right where it had burst — with Huhu. It settled into his chest and filled his throat with something warm and strange.

Huhu opened his mouth, and out came the most extraordinary sound the forest had ever heard. It was part whistle, part howl, part song — a sound that echoed across the valleys and bounced off the mountains and made every creature in the forest stop and listen.

*"Hoo-hoo-hoo-HOO!"*

## The Morning Promise

The Moon smiled. "Huhu," she said, "you received the largest piece because you live closest to the sky. But with this gift comes a responsibility. Every morning, before the sun rises, you must sing. Your song will wake the forest. Your song will tell every creature that a new day has begun."

"Every morning?" said Huhu. "Even when it rains?"

"*Especially* when it rains," said the Moon. "That's when the forest needs music the most."

And so, to this very day, if you go to the forests of Assam, Arunachal Pradesh, Meghalaya, or Nagaland just before sunrise, you will hear it — the hoolock gibbon's song. *Hoo-hoo-hoo-HOO!* Loud and clear and beautiful, echoing through the mist.

It is the oldest song in the forest. It is the Moon's gift. And it has not missed a single morning in ten thousand years.

*The end.*

# 10 Amazing Facts About Northeast India That Will Surprise You!

> Did you know that the world's largest river island is in Assam? Or that Meghalaya has living bridges made of tree roots? Here are 10 fun facts about our incredible region.

## Fact 1: The World's Largest River Island

**Majuli** in Assam is the largest river island in the world! It sits in the middle of the mighty Brahmaputra river and is home to about 1.7 lakh people. It has ancient monasteries, beautiful wetlands, and its own unique culture. But the river is slowly making it smaller every year — which is why people are working hard to protect it.

## Fact 2: Living Root Bridges

In **Meghalaya**, the Khasi and Jaintia people grow bridges out of living tree roots! They train the roots of rubber fig trees across rivers, and after 15-20 years, the roots grow strong enough to walk on. Some of these bridges are over 500 years old and can hold 50 people at once. They actually get stronger as they age!

## Fact 3: The Wettest Place on Earth

**Mawsynram** in Meghalaya holds the world record for the most rainfall — over 11,871 millimetres every year! That's like filling a 4-storey building with water. The people there use special grass umbrellas called *knups* that cover their whole body.

## Fact 4: One-Horned Rhinos

**Kaziranga National Park** in Assam is home to two-thirds of the world's one-horned rhinoceroses. There are now over 3,000 of them! These incredible animals can weigh as much as a car and run as fast as 55 km/h. Despite their tough-looking armour, they are actually gentle plant-eaters.

## Fact 5: The Cleanest Village in Asia

**Mawlynnong** in Meghalaya has been called the cleanest village in Asia. Every person in the village — including children — helps keep the streets spotless. They have bamboo dustbins everywhere and even a living root bridge right next to the village.

## Fact 6: More Than 200 Languages

Northeast India is one of the most linguistically diverse places on Earth. Over **200 languages** are spoken across the eight states! Some of these languages are spoken by fewer than 1,000 people, making them very rare and precious.

## Fact 7: Tea Capital of the World

Assam produces more tea than most *countries*! The tea gardens of Assam cover over 3 lakh hectares and produce about 700 million kilograms of tea every year. That's enough to make roughly 280 billion cups of tea — one for every person on Earth, 35 times over!

## Fact 8: The Only Floating National Park

**Keibul Lamjao** in Manipur is the world's only floating national park. It floats on Loktak Lake and is home to the endangered Sangai deer — also called the "dancing deer" because of the way it walks on the floating grasslands.

## Fact 9: India's Oldest Oil Well

Asia's first commercially drilled oil well was in **Digboi, Assam**, way back in 1890! The refinery there is still working after more than 130 years, making it one of the oldest functioning refineries in the world.

## Fact 10: The Land of Festivals

Northeast India celebrates more festivals than you can count! **Bihu** in Assam, **Hornbill Festival** in Nagaland, **Wangala** in Meghalaya, **Losar** in Arunachal Pradesh, **Kut** in Manipur — almost every month has a celebration. The people of Northeast India believe there's always a reason to dance, sing, and share a meal together.

# Tejimola — The Girl Who Became a Plant

> One of Assam's most beloved folktales, Tejimola is the story of a kind girl whose spirit refused to be silenced, no matter how many times cruelty tried to bury it.

## The Kind Girl

In a village by the river, there lived a girl named **Tejimola**. She was kind to every creature — she fed the sparrows, she sang to the fish, and she never passed a person without smiling. Her father loved her dearly, but her stepmother did not.

Tejimola's stepmother was jealous of how much everyone loved the girl. "Why does the whole village smile when she walks by?" the stepmother muttered. "Nobody smiles for me."

## The Cruel Plan

One day, when Tejimola's father went away on a long journey, the stepmother decided to get rid of the girl. She was so cruel that she harmed poor Tejimola and buried her in the garden, thinking that would be the end.

But Tejimola's spirit was as stubborn as her kindness. From the spot where she was buried, a beautiful **tulsi plant** grew overnight — green and fragrant and reaching for the sun.

The stepmother was furious. She pulled out the tulsi plant and threw it in the river.

## The Spirit That Would Not Die

But from the riverbank, a **gourd vine** grew — lush and green, bearing the most beautiful gourds anyone had ever seen. The stepmother tore it down.

From the fallen vine, a **lotus** bloomed in the pond — so radiant that the whole village came to see it. The stepmother plucked it.

From the ground where the lotus fell, a **champa tree** grew — tall and golden-flowered, filling the air with perfume.

No matter what the stepmother did, Tejimola kept coming back — as a plant, as a flower, as a tree. Each time more beautiful than before.

## The Return

When Tejimola's father finally returned, he sat under the champa tree to rest. The tree bent its branches and gently touched his face, and the wind carried a whisper: *"Father, I am here. I never left."*

The father understood. He wept, and his tears watered the roots of the tree. The tree bloomed brighter than it ever had.

The village learned the truth, and the cruel stepmother was sent away forever.

## What the Story Teaches Us

Tejimola's story tells us that kindness cannot be destroyed. You can bury it, pull it out, throw it away — but it grows back, every time, in a new form. The people of Assam have told this story for hundreds of years because it reminds us: goodness is the one thing in this world that is truly impossible to kill.

*The end.*

# The Golden Deer of Kamakhya Hill

> High on Nilachal Hill, where the ancient Kamakhya temple stands, there once lived a golden deer that granted one wish to anyone pure of heart.

## The Temple on the Hill

On top of **Nilachal Hill** in Guwahati, there stands an ancient temple that has watched over the Brahmaputra valley for thousands of years. The people call it **Kamakhya**, and they say it is one of the most sacred places in all of India.

But long before the temple was built, when the hill was covered in wild forests and the only visitors were monkeys and eagles, a **golden deer** lived there. Its coat shone like the sun, and its eyes sparkled like the stars reflected in the river below.

## The Gift

The golden deer had a special power: it could grant one wish to anyone whose heart was truly pure. But there was a catch — the deer could see inside your heart. If your wish was selfish, the deer would simply vanish. Only those who wished for others could see their wish come true.

Many people climbed the hill to find the deer. Kings came with armies. Merchants came with gold. Scholars came with clever words. But the deer vanished every time, because each of them wished for something for themselves.

## The Farmer's Daughter

One day, a farmer's daughter named **Junali** climbed the hill. She was barefoot and tired, and she carried nothing but a handful of rice.

"Why have you come?" asked the deer, appearing before her.

"The river flooded our village," said Junali. "The crops are gone. The people are hungry. I've brought this rice to share with whoever lives on this hill, but I see it's only you. Would you like some?"

She held out her handful of rice to the golden deer.

The deer smiled — if deer can smile — and said, *"You came to a wishing deer, and instead of wishing, you offered to share. That is the purest heart I have ever seen. What do you truly want?"*

Junali thought for a moment. "I wish the river would be kinder to my village."

## The Wish

The golden deer stamped its hoof, and from the spot where it struck, a spring of clear water bubbled up — water that flowed down the hill and into the village, nourishing the fields even when the Brahmaputra flooded.

The spring still flows today. If you visit Kamakhya temple and walk to the eastern side of the hill, you'll find it — cool and clear, trickling over moss-covered stones.

Nobody has seen the golden deer since. But people say that on full moon nights, if you sit very quietly on Nilachal Hill, you can hear the faint click of golden hooves on stone.

*The end.*

# Why the Brahmaputra Is Always Angry

> The Brahmaputra is one of the mightiest rivers in the world. But why is it always in a hurry, always flooding, always changing course? An old story says it's because of a promise made long ago.

## The Son of Brahma

Long ago, when the world was new and the mountains were still growing, the god **Brahma** created a river and named it after himself — **Brahmaputra**, which means "Son of Brahma."

"You will be the mightiest river in the East," Brahma told his son. "You will carry the water from the Himalayas to the sea. You will nourish the land and the people who live along your banks."

The young Brahmaputra was proud and excited. "I will be the greatest river ever!" he said.

## The Race

But the Brahmaputra had a rival — the river **Ganga**, who flowed through the plains of India and was loved by everyone. "Everyone sings songs about Ganga," complained the Brahmaputra. "Nobody writes poems about me."

So the Brahmaputra challenged Ganga to a race. "Let us see who can reach the sea first!" he said.

Ganga, who was wise and calm, said, "A river is not meant to race. A river is meant to nourish."

But the Brahmaputra wouldn't listen. He was already running.

## The Rush

He crashed through the mountains of Tibet. He smashed through the gorges of Arunachal Pradesh — creating the deepest canyon in the world. He roared across the plains of Assam, flooding everything in his path, changing direction whenever he felt like it, swallowing islands and villages and forests.

"Slow down!" cried the fish.

"Wait for us!" cried the dolphins.

"You're destroying our homes!" cried the people.

But the Brahmaputra was too focused on winning. He kept running, wider and wilder, until he finally reached the Bay of Bengal — and discovered that Ganga had been there all along, sitting quietly, waiting.

## The Lesson

"How did you get here first?" gasped the Brahmaputra.

"I didn't race," said Ganga. "I flowed. There's a difference."

The Brahmaputra was embarrassed. He realized that in his hurry, he had destroyed the very land he was supposed to nourish. He tried to slow down, but by then it was too late — he had carved his path too wide, too wild, too unpredictable.

And so, to this day, the Brahmaputra is always restless. Always flooding. Always changing course. Not because he's angry at us — but because he's angry at himself, for choosing speed over kindness.

The people of Assam have learned to live with his moods. They build their houses on stilts. They plant rice that grows in floodwater. They teach their children to swim before they teach them to walk. Because when you live with a river that never learned to slow down, you learn to be patient enough for both of you.

*The end.*

# The Weaver Girl of Sualkuchi

> In the silk village of Sualkuchi, a young weaver discovers that the most beautiful patterns come not from copying nature, but from listening to it.

## The Village of Looms

In the village of **Sualkuchi**, on the north bank of the Brahmaputra, every house has a loom. The sound of weaving — *thak-thak, thak-thak* — is as constant as birdsong. The people here have been weaving silk for over a thousand years, and their mekhela chadors are famous across all of India.

In this village lived a girl named **Rupahi**, who could weave faster than anyone. Her fingers flew across the loom like swallows across the sky. But Rupahi had a problem: her patterns were beautiful, but they were always the same. Flowers. Peacocks. Elephants. The same designs her grandmother had taught her.

"I want to weave something new," said Rupahi. "Something nobody has ever seen."

## The Search

Rupahi went to the river to look for new patterns. She watched the water and tried to weave its ripples — but the silk looked like wrinkled cloth.

She went to the forest and tried to weave the pattern of sunlight through leaves — but the silk looked like a jumble of yellow spots.

She went to the hills and tried to weave the clouds — but the silk looked like a blank white sheet.

"I can see the beauty," said Rupahi, frustrated, "but I can't weave it."

## The Old Silkworm

That night, as Rupahi sat sadly beside her loom, a silkworm crawled onto her hand. It was very old — its body was wrinkled and slow.

*"You are trying to copy what you see,"* said the silkworm, in a voice like rustling leaves. *"But the best patterns don't come from the eyes. They come from listening."*

"Listening to what?" asked Rupahi.

*"Close your eyes. Listen to the river. Not with your ears — with your fingers."*

## The New Pattern

Rupahi closed her eyes and placed her hands on the loom. She listened. Not to the sound of the river, but to its *feeling* — the way it moved, the way it carried things gently, the way it turned and turned and never stopped.

Her fingers began to move. The loom spoke — *thak-thak, thak-thak* — and a pattern emerged that Rupahi had never seen before. It wasn't a flower or a peacock or an elephant. It was something that had no name — a design that looked like the feeling of water, the memory of wind, the dream of moonlight on silk.

When she opened her eyes, the cloth on the loom was the most beautiful thing she had ever woven.

The whole village came to see it. "What is this pattern called?" they asked.

Rupahi smiled. "I don't know. I didn't design it. I just listened, and the loom told me what to weave."

From that day on, the weavers of Sualkuchi added a new tradition: before starting any new design, they sit quietly at the loom, close their eyes, and listen. The best silk in Assam, they say, is woven not with the hands but with the heart.

*The end.*

# The Elephant and the Ant — A Mishing Tale

> From the Mishing community of Assam comes this story about the biggest and the smallest creatures in the forest, and what happened when they had to cross a river together.

## The Biggest and the Smallest

In the forests along the **Subansiri river**, where the Mishing people have lived for centuries on their beautiful bamboo houses raised on stilts, there once lived the biggest elephant in all of Assam and the smallest ant.

The elephant's name was **Dolung**, and he was so large that when he walked through the forest, the trees bowed out of his way. The ant's name was **Miri**, and she was so small that she could ride on a grain of rice.

Dolung was proud of his size. "I am the mightiest creature in the forest!" he would say, stomping his feet so hard the ground shook.

Miri never said anything about her size. She was too busy carrying food, building tunnels, and helping her colony survive.

## The Flood

One monsoon, the Subansiri river rose higher than anyone could remember. The water poured into the forest, covering the paths and the meadows. All the animals needed to cross to higher ground.

Dolung laughed. "This is nothing for me!" He waded into the water, which only came up to his belly. "Everyone else will drown, but I will walk across like a king!"

But as Dolung walked deeper, the river's current grew stronger. The mud beneath his feet was soft, and his enormous weight made him sink deeper and deeper. Soon, he was stuck — his great legs buried in mud, the water rising around him.

"Help!" cried the mighty elephant. "I'm stuck!"

The other animals were too small or too weak to pull him out. The deer tried. The wild buffalo tried. Even the bear tried. Nobody could move him.

## The Smallest Helper

Then Miri the ant appeared on a floating leaf. "I can help," she said.

The animals laughed. "You? You're the smallest creature in the forest! How can you help the biggest?"

But Miri didn't argue. She called her colony — ten thousand ants — and they marched down into the mud beneath Dolung's feet. Working together, tiny grain by tiny grain, they moved the mud away from his legs. It took them all day and all night, but by morning, Dolung could move again.

He pulled himself free and walked to high ground, carrying Miri and her entire colony safely on his back.

## The Promise

"I was wrong," said Dolung quietly. "Being big doesn't mean being mighty. You saved me with something I don't have — patience, teamwork, and the willingness to do a thousand small things when one big thing won't work."

From that day on, Dolung and Miri became friends. And the Mishing people, who watched this happen from their stilt houses above the flood, made it into a saying that they still use today: *"The river does not care how big you are. It only cares how well you work together."*

*The end.*

# The Boy Who Talked to Clouds — A Khasi Story

> In the hills of Meghalaya, where it rains more than anywhere else on Earth, a boy discovers why the clouds are always crying — and what they really need is a friend.

## The Wettest Place

In **Cherrapunji**, in the hills of Meghalaya, it rains almost every day. The people there are used to it — they carry special grass umbrellas called *knups* that cover their whole body, and their houses are built to let the water run off like a duck's back.

But a boy named **Bah Kit** always wondered: why does it rain so much here? His mother said it was because Meghalaya means "the abode of clouds." His teacher said it was because of geography and monsoon winds. But Bah Kit thought there must be a deeper reason.

## The Climb

One morning, when the clouds were sitting so low on the hills that you could almost touch them, Bah Kit climbed to the highest point he could find — a rocky ledge above his village — and reached out his hand.

His fingers disappeared into the cloud. It was cold and wet and soft, like touching a dream.

"Hello?" he whispered.

*"Hello,"* said the cloud.

Bah Kit nearly fell off the ledge.

## Why Clouds Cry

"Why are you always crying?" asked Bah Kit, once he'd caught his breath.

*"I'm not crying,"* said the cloud, sounding a little offended. *"I'm giving."*

"Giving what?"

*"Water. The rivers need it. The trees need it. The rice paddies need it. Your mother needs it to cook and wash. Even you need it — you drank some this morning."*

"But don't you get tired of giving all the time?"

The cloud was quiet for a long moment. Then it said, very softly: *"Sometimes. Sometimes I wish someone would give something to me."*

"What do you want?" asked Bah Kit.

*"Company. I travel across the whole sky, from the ocean to the mountains. I see everything but I can never stop. I give water to everyone but nobody ever just... talks to me."*

## The Promise

Bah Kit thought about this. Then he sat down on the ledge, right inside the cloud, and said: "Tell me about your journey. Where did you come from today?"

And the cloud told him. It told him about the Bay of Bengal, warm and blue and endless. About flying over Bangladesh and seeing a million green fields. About hitting the Khasi Hills and feeling the cool air squeeze the water out of it like wringing a towel.

Bah Kit listened for hours. And when the cloud finally drifted away, it left behind the clearest, most beautiful sunset Cherrapunji had seen in years.

From that day on, Bah Kit climbed the ledge every morning to talk to the clouds. And the people of the village noticed something strange: it still rained just as much, but somehow, the rain felt gentler.

The Khasi elders say this is because when you listen to the rain — really listen, not just hear — it falls softer. As if it knows someone cares.

*The end.*

# How Majuli Island Was Born

> The world's largest river island didn't appear by accident. According to an old tale, it was created by a turtle, a crane, and a promise.

## The Argument

Long ago, the **Brahmaputra** river was even wider than it is today — so wide that you couldn't see from one bank to the other. The creatures who lived in the river and the creatures who lived on the banks never met, because nobody could cross.

One day, a **turtle** named **Kasim** and a **crane** named **Bogi** got into an argument.

"Water is better than land!" said Kasim the turtle. "In water, you float. On land, you just... sit there."

"Land is better than water!" said Bogi the crane. "On land, you can build things. In water, everything washes away."

They argued for three days and three nights, until they were both exhausted and neither had won.

## The Compromise

"What if," said Kasim slowly, "there was a place that was both? A piece of land, right in the middle of the water?"

"That's impossible," said Bogi.

"Is it?" said Kasim. And he dove to the bottom of the Brahmaputra — down, down, down through the muddy water — and brought up a mouthful of sand. He placed it on the surface of the river.

The sand sank.

He tried again. And again. And again. Each time, the sand sank.

Bogi watched from above. "Give up," she said. "Some things aren't meant to be."

But Kasim was a turtle, and turtles don't give up. They just slow down.

## Ten Thousand Trips

For **one hundred years**, Kasim dove to the bottom and brought up sand. Grain by grain. Mouthful by mouthful. The pile grew — slowly, impossibly slowly — until one morning, a tiny patch of sand poked above the surface of the river.

Bogi flew down and stood on it. Her feet stayed dry.

"It's land!" she cried. "It's land in the middle of water!"

And then she did something that surprised even herself — she flew to the forests on the bank, picked up a seed in her beak, and planted it in the sand. The seed grew into a tree. The tree's roots held the sand in place. More sand collected. More seeds arrived, carried by wind and birds.

Within another hundred years, the tiny patch of sand had become a lush, green island — the largest river island in the world.

## The Island's Name

The people who eventually came to live on the island named it **Majuli**. They built sattras — beautiful monasteries where they sang and danced and preserved the culture of Assam. They grew rice and mustard and bananas. They lived between water and land, just as Kasim and Bogi had imagined.

And if you visit Majuli today, you'll see turtles sunning themselves on the banks and cranes standing in the shallows, living side by side, as if remembering an old friendship that built an island out of patience and sand.

*The end.*

# The Bamboo Flute of Nagaland

> A young Naga boy carves a flute from bamboo and discovers it plays a different song for every person who listens — the song of what they need to hear most.

## The Boy and the Bamboo

In a village high in the hills of **Nagaland**, where the clouds walk through the streets like neighbours and the forests are so green they seem to glow, a boy named **Ato** found a perfect piece of bamboo.

It was straight and smooth and golden, and when Ato tapped it, it rang like a bell. "This bamboo wants to be a flute," he said.

Ato's grandfather, who had been a warrior in his youth and was now the gentlest person in the village, taught him how to carve it — where to place the holes, how deep to cut, how to smooth the edges with river sand.

"A good flute," said his grandfather, "doesn't make the sound you want. It makes the sound the listener needs."

Ato didn't understand what this meant. But he finished the flute and put it to his lips.

## The First Song

When Ato played, the sound that came out was not what he expected. He was trying to play a happy song, but the flute played something else — something slow and gentle, like a lullaby.

His mother, who was sitting nearby, began to cry. "That's the song my mother used to sing to me," she said, "before she passed away. I had forgotten it completely."

Ato was confused. He hadn't played that song. He didn't even know it.

## The Flute's Secret

He tried again in the village square. This time, the flute played a brave, drumming rhythm — and old **Uncle Vizo**, who had been sad for months, stood up and laughed. "That's the war song from when I was young! I feel strong again!"

Ato played for the children, and the flute made a silly, bouncing melody that made everyone dance. He played for the village elder, and the flute made a deep, peaceful hum that helped her fall asleep for the first time in weeks.

Every person heard a different song from the same flute.

## The Grandfather's Explanation

"Grandfather," said Ato, "this flute is magic. It plays a different song for everyone."

His grandfather smiled. "It's not magic, boy. It's bamboo. Bamboo is hollow — it has nothing of its own inside. So when you blow into it, it fills with whatever the listener needs. An empty heart makes the truest music, because it has room for other people's songs."

Ato thought about this for a long time. Then he said, "Is that why you're the gentlest person in the village, Grandfather? Because you're like the bamboo — you don't fill yourself up with your own noise?"

His grandfather laughed — a big, warm laugh that sounded, just for a moment, exactly like the flute.

*The end.*

# The Dancing Deer of Loktak Lake

> On the floating grasslands of Manipur's Loktak Lake lives the Sangai deer — the only deer in the world that dances. But it wasn't always a dancer. This is the story of how it learned.

## The Floating World

In the heart of **Manipur**, there is a lake unlike any other. **Loktak Lake** is covered with floating islands of grass called *phumdis* — thick mats of soil and vegetation that drift across the water like green clouds.

On these floating islands lives the **Sangai deer** — the rarest deer in the world, found nowhere else on Earth. The Manipuri people call it the "dancing deer" because of the delicate way it walks on the soft, bouncing phumdis, each step looking like a graceful dance move.

But the Sangai was not always a dancer.

## The Clumsy Fawn

Long ago, the first Sangai deer lived on solid ground like every other deer. They were strong and fast, but they were also — and this is embarrassing — incredibly clumsy. They tripped over roots. They stumbled on rocks. They were the worst runners in the whole forest.

The tigers knew this, and they chased the Sangai constantly. "Run to the lake!" the mother deer told her fawn one day, as a tiger's roar shook the trees. "The tiger can't follow us onto the water!"

The fawn ran — stumbling and tripping — to the edge of Loktak Lake and leaped onto a floating phumdi.

## Learning to Dance

The phumdi bounced under the fawn's feet like a mattress. She fell. She got up. She fell again. The surface was soft and springy — nothing like the hard ground she was used to.

"I can't walk here!" she cried.

"Then don't walk," said her mother. *"Dance."*

"I don't know how to dance!"

"Neither did I, when I first came here. But the lake teaches you. Step lightly. Feel the bounce. Let the ground move you instead of fighting it."

The fawn tried. Step. Bounce. Step. Bounce. At first she was awkward, but slowly — over days, over weeks — her steps became lighter, softer, more graceful. She learned to float on her feet the way the phumdis floated on the water.

And by the time the tiger found his way to the lake's edge, the fawn was dancing across the floating islands so beautifully that even the tiger stopped to watch.

## The Gift of the Lake

Generation after generation, the Sangai deer lived on the floating phumdis. Their legs grew slender. Their hooves grew wide and soft. Their every movement became a dance — because on a floating world, you either learn grace or you sink.

The Manipuri people, who are among the finest dancers in all of India, say they learned their first dance steps by watching the Sangai. The classical dance form of **Manipuri** — gentle, flowing, never harsh — echoes the way the deer moves on the floating grass.

Today, there are fewer than 300 Sangai deer left. The people of Manipur protect them fiercely, because they know that when the dancing deer disappears, a piece of the lake's soul goes with it.

*The end.*

# The Bridge That Grew — A Meghalaya Wonder

> What if instead of building a bridge, you could grow one? In Meghalaya, the Khasi people have been doing exactly that for 500 years. This is the story of the very first living root bridge.

## The Problem

Five hundred years ago, in the deep valleys of the **Khasi Hills** in Meghalaya, the people had a problem. The monsoon rivers were too wild to cross. Every year, wooden bridges were washed away by the rain. Stone was too heavy to carry down the steep hills. Rope rotted in the wet air.

The rivers cut villages off from each other for months during the rainy season. Children couldn't go to school. Families couldn't visit each other. Markets couldn't open.

"We need a bridge that the river cannot destroy," said the village elder, a woman named **Ka Ri**.

"There is no such thing," said the men who had built and lost bridges for generations.

"Then we must make one," said Ka Ri. And she looked up at the giant *Ficus elastica* trees that grew along the riverbanks — rubber fig trees with roots so strong they could crack stone.

## The Idea

Ka Ri noticed that the tree's roots were alive — they grew, they moved, they reached out for soil and water. "What if," she thought, "we could teach the roots to grow across the river?"

She took a hollowed-out betel nut trunk, split it lengthwise, and laid it across the river like a guide. Then she gently placed the young aerial roots of a Ficus tree into the trunk, pointing them toward the other side.

"Now we wait," she said.

"How long?" asked the villagers.

"A generation. Maybe two."

## The Growing

The roots grew. Slowly — just a few inches a month — they crept through the hollowed trunk, reaching toward the other bank. Ka Ri tended them like children. She guided roots that wandered off course. She braided thin roots together to make them stronger. She waited.

Ka Ri did not live to see the bridge completed. Neither did her daughter. But her granddaughter, **Ka Shan**, was the first person to walk across the living root bridge — a bridge made entirely of roots, alive and growing, stronger than any bridge made by human hands.

The bridge had no nails, no cement, no ropes. It was a living thing that drank water from the river it crossed and grew thicker and stronger with every passing year.

## Five Hundred Years Later

Today, there are over 100 living root bridges in Meghalaya. Some are strong enough to hold 50 people at once. The most famous one, in the village of **Nongriat**, has a double level — two bridges stacked one above the other, built by training roots for centuries.

Scientists from around the world come to study them. Engineers call them "the most sustainable bridges ever created." But the Khasi people just call them what they are: gifts from the past to the future.

Because that's what Ka Ri understood, all those years ago: the best things we build are not for ourselves. They are for the grandchildren we will never meet.

*The end.*
